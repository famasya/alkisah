## 1. Developmental Stage Theory (Jean Piaget)

A common foundation for writing children’s books comes from **developmental psychology**, especially the work of Jean Piaget.

Piaget proposed that children think differently at different ages. Good children’s books align with these stages.

| Age  | Stage                | Writing implications                     |
| ---- | -------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| 0–2  | Sensorimotor         | Visual-heavy books, textures, repetition |
| 2–7  | Preoperational       | Simple stories, fantasy, strong visuals  |
| 7–11 | Concrete operational | Logical plots, problem solving           |
| 11+  | Formal operational   | Complex themes, moral ambiguity          |

Example:
Picture books for ages 3–5 often rely on **repetition and predictable structure** because children at that stage learn through pattern recognition.

## 2. Vygotsky’s Scaffolding Theory

Another important framework comes from Lev Vygotsky.

His idea: children learn best in the **Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)**.
This means stories should be:

- slightly above the child’s current ability
- but still understandable with context

Implication for writing:

- introduce **one new concept at a time**
- use **context clues and illustrations**
- allow the reader to infer meaning

## 3. Narrative Structure for Children

Children’s stories often follow a simplified **story grammar**:

1. Character introduction
2. Goal or problem
3. Attempts to solve
4. Twist or obstacle
5. Resolution
6. Emotional lesson

A widely cited storytelling framework for children is derived from **Propp’s narrative structure**, proposed by Vladimir Propp, which analyzed patterns in folktales.

Typical patterns include:

- hero leaves home
- encounters challenge
- receives help
- defeats problem
- returns transformed

Many children’s books unconsciously follow this structure.

---

## 4. The “Rule of Three”

A common practical storytelling principle:

Children understand patterns easily when events occur **three times**.

Example pattern:

1. First attempt fails
2. Second attempt fails
3. Third attempt succeeds

Classic books like **The Three Little Pigs** follow this structure.

## 5. Picture–Text Interaction Theory

In picture books, the relationship between **illustration and text** is critical.

Scholar Perry Nodelman describes several relationships:

| Type          | Description                        |
| ------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Redundant     | Image repeats text                 |
| Complementary | Image adds information             |
| Counterpoint  | Image contradicts text             |
| Parallel      | Image and text tell separate parts |

Modern picture books often rely on **complementary storytelling**.

---

## 6. Moral Development (Kohlberg)

Another influence is moral psychology from Lawrence Kohlberg.

Young children typically operate at **pre-conventional morality**:

- actions judged by reward or punishment

So early children’s stories often include:

- clear consequences
- simple right vs wrong
- emotional empathy

Older children can handle **moral complexity**.

## Practical Design Rules (Modern Children’s Books)

From publishers and editors, several empirical guidelines exist:

**Picture books (3–6 years)**

- 400–800 words
- strong rhythm and repetition
- one core idea
- visual storytelling

**Early readers**

- short sentences
- predictable vocabulary
- clear structure

**Middle grade**

- stronger plot
- character development
- internal conflict
